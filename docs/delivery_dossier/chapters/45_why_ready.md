# Chapter 45 — Why we are ready for market

> *In one paragraph.* CIHRMS is ready to onboard its first paying customer. Not feature-complete for a Ministry-wide rollout — Chapter 44 documents the residual gaps, and Chapter 46 sequences the closing work — but ready for a pilot. The case has three parts: that the shipped surface area matches what mid-market HRMS buyers actually purchase (Claim 1), that the build is engineered with enough rigour to survive a buyer's IT review (Claim 2), and that the finance and statutory stack is materially ahead of the HRMS market we sell into (Claim 3). Every paragraph that follows cites the chapter or the audit row that backs it; there are no claims here that don't have evidence elsewhere in the dossier.

## Claim 1 — Commercial mid-market parity

CIHRMS ships thirty-five module chapters. The audit (`build/module_audit.json`) records twelve as **fully shipped**, twenty-one as **shipped with gaps documented**, and two as deliberate stubs. Every one of the thirty-three with working surface is reachable from the sidebar, has a Pest feature test, and is walked module-by-module in Part I of this dossier (Ch 3 through Ch 35). For a mid-market HRMS buyer comparing CIHRMS against SeamlessHR, Aruti, BambooHR, or a "Workday Lite" footprint, the module list resolves to parity on the core HR concerns and to a clear lead on the compliance and finance concerns. The rest of this section walks the nine functional areas a buyer's HR director will check first.

**Employee management.** Chapter 3 is the master record — auto-generated `SID-NNNNNN` staff IDs and `CIHRM-NNNN` employee numbers, six-tab detail page (Overview / Documents / Leave / Tickets / Payroll / Benefits), salary visibility gated at the resource layer via `employees.view_salary`, Ghana Card / SSNIT / TIN masked to last-four with click-to-copy, soft delete preserving audit trail, transactional create with benefits enrolment, `EmployeeCreated` event fanning to analytics, welcome mail, and AI-summary listeners. The Profile Portal (Ch 32) gives the employee a tabbed self-service face onto the same row. Ten of the eleven shipped items in the audit are unqualified; the two recorded partials are free-text position (Phase 1 swaps to FK) and lax phone validation. Neither prevents a pilot.

**Leave.** Chapter 4 ships seven leave types priced against Labour Act 651 (Annual, Sick, Maternity, Paternity, Emergency, Study, Unpaid), the Apply panel, the Approvals Desk, and a Leave Calendar layered with Ghana public holidays. Balance increments are row-locked with `lockForUpdate` — the double-spend window is closed. `LeaveRequested` and `LeaveStatusUpdated` events drive analytics and the manager-DM listener (Slack / Teams / WhatsApp via channel preferences). The known partials are 21-day default balance seeding (per-grade entitlements come with Establishment in Phase 2) and supporting-document upload (widget present, storage hook deferred). A pilot customer with a standard annual-leave policy uses this module on day one.

**Performance management.** Chapter 6 ships cycles, goals with mood flagging (green / amber / red auto-flips At Risk), 1–5 reviews on overall / performance / potential, the 9-box matrix, PSC Performance Contracts with weighted KPIs and dual signatures, calibration sessions with the Ghana 10/25/40/20/5 default distribution and dual-control apply, and PIPs with check-ins, extensions, and closure outcomes that satisfy Labour Act §63. The end-cycle weighted-achievement is `Σ(weight × score) / Σ(weight)`, and the Performance↔Pay loop is wired through `StepIncrementService` gating step bumps on review ≥ 3.0. The audit records six gaps as Phase 1 enhancements (360 campaign engine, KPI cascade UI, per-manager dashboards); none are blocking for a first commercial pilot, where the unblocked surface is the entire cycle.

**Learning and development.** Chapter 7 ships the course catalogue, My Learning, the Skills Matrix heatmap with CSV export, and the external certifications register (ACCA, ISACA, etc.) with `verification_url`. Course completion grants skills automatically via `GrantSkillsOnCourseCompletion`. A scheduled artisan command `governance:certification-reminders` fires 30 / 15 / 7-day reminder events. What L&D does **not** ship in v1 is in-system content delivery — no video player, no SCORM, no quiz engine. The audit calls this out cleanly: Phase 3 builds the content layer. For a pilot customer using CIHRMS as the system-of-record for course catalogue, enrolment tracking, and certification expiry — which is exactly what 80% of mid-market HRMS buyers use their L&D module for — Chapter 7 is sufficient.

**Recruitment.** Chapter 9 ships job posting CRUD, a public `/careers/{job}` apply form (no auth, 5/min throttle), a 6-column kanban (Applied → Shortlisted → Interviewed → Offered → Hired → Rejected), offer-letter PDFs via DomPDF, and an `ESignProvider` contract with both Zoho Sign and DocuSign drivers wired through `ESignWebhookController`. The audit calls out a real wart: the `ApplicantHired` event is *defined and listened-for* but never dispatched, so the auto-creation of Employee + User from a signed envelope still requires HR to manually trigger the conversion. This is fixed in PR-track work in Phase 1, and is the single most explicit "wired-but-not-dispatched" item in the audit. A pilot that uses recruitment as a hiring funnel + offer-letter dispatcher gets value today; the auto-conversion lands soon after.

**Off-boarding.** Chapter 10 ships the full Initiate → Clearance → Settlement → Approval → Termination lifecycle with the 12-item default checklist (IT, Finance, HR, Stores, Security, Handover, Pension), Labour Act §31 severance computation, dual-control settlement approval (`calculated_by ≠ approved_by`, enforced as `DomainException`), `2fa:fresh` middleware on approve and complete, automatic loan-balance netting via `OffboardingService::closeOutstandingLoans`, and `OffboardingCompleted` event with webhook fan-out. The audit's red flag — account deprovisioning is checklist-only, not enforced on the User row — is a Phase 1 item and is explicitly tracked in Chapter 10's roadmap section. Pilot customers manage exits cleanly today; the auto-disable lands in the same phase as the rest of Phase 1.

**Payroll.** Chapter 19 is the most-engineered module in the build. Period-locked dual-approval runs (Draft → Calculated → Approved → Paid → Reversed). `PayeCalculator` against the 2026 7-band monthly bracket with effective-dated `tax_brackets`. `SsnitCalculator` with employee 5.5%, employer 13%, NHIA 2.5% split, MIE cap GHS 61,000. `Tier2Calculator` 5% employer to NPRA-licensed trustees (five seeded). `OvertimeCalculator` implementing Labour Act §35 (1.5x weekday, 2x rest/holiday). Two ghost-worker gates — `hasUsableIdentity` (Ghana Card) and zero-attendance check — protect the run. Dual-control approval at policy + service + middleware. Five statutory return generators (PAYE CSV, SSNIT Tier-1, NHIA, per-trustee Tier-2, GhIPSS bank file). And the IPPD2/IPPD3 and GIFMIS journal exporters, which Claim 3 returns to. Every standards row in the audit is "met": Income Tax Act 896, Pensions Act 766, NHIA Act 852, SSNIT Act PNDCL 247, Labour Act 651 §35, E-Levy Act 1075, PFM Act 921 §11, ISO 30414 §4.6, NIA Act 707.

**Finance.** Chapter 20 ships F1 through F5 in full: Chart of Accounts with five GL families and the Finance Hub showing live cash position, AP outstanding, and statutory compliance band; F2 Vendors + AP Invoices + AP Payments with `JournalPostingService` as sole `gl_account_balances` mutator; F3 Customers + AR Invoices + AR Receipts + Customer Statements with aging and a clean write-off path; F4 Paystack hosted-checkout with HMAC-SHA512 signed webhooks and bulk refunds; F5 Bank Reconciliation with CSV / OFX 2.x / MT940 SWIFT parsers and a three-tier matcher. Dual-control on every invoice approval. `2fa:fresh` on every cash-moving endpoint. `SequenceService` on every Finance reference (PR #21 closed the race condition). And the double-entry invariants — debits = credits at posting, balance = natural sum of posted lines, source reconstructable both ways — are encoded in the service layer, not "expected" by convention. This is what makes Claim 3 a sentence and not a paragraph.

**Audit and provenance.** Chapter 24 ships the tamper-evident chain in production: `AuditTrail` middleware on every mutating route, `WriteAuditLog` job dispatched async on the `audit` queue, SHA-256 hash chain with `chain_position` + `previous_hash` + canonical-JSON `row_hash`, `lockForUpdate` on the latest row to give total order under concurrent writes, `audit:verify-chain` walking the chain with `hash_equals` constant-time comparison, and a nightly 03:00 schedule firing `AuditChainBroken` to super_admins if the chain breaks. The Auditor-General Report Pack (Ch 24, also surfaced in Ch 31) emits 14 artefacts in a sealed ZIP with a `MANIFEST.md` carrying SHA-256 of every file. The audit records this as "met" against DPA §28, ISO/IEC 27001:2022 A.12.4, NIST SP 800-92 §4, Auditor-General Act 584 §13, Whistleblower Act 720 §10, and PFM Act 921 §52. The Phase 2 widening (action vocabulary, per-row detail page, audit-log foreign keys on high-stakes tables) is the cleanup, not the foundation.

In aggregate: a buyer running the standard mid-market RFP feature checklist against CIHRMS finds the answer is "yes" on every line except the small handful that Chapter 44 and Chapter 46 already name. That is the parity claim.

## Claim 2 — Engineering rigour

A buyer's IT shop will not stop at the feature checklist. The serious procurement gates are the ones a CTO or InfoSec director walks: how was it built, how is it tested, how is it operated, and what does the evidence look like when someone asks. CIHRMS holds up at each of those gates because the build was deliberately steered to.

**The V2 audit punch-list, fully closed.** In the week ending 2026-05-24, a comprehensive market-readiness audit was run across the whole application — three parallel static-analysis sweeps surfaced ~140 issues across backend reliability, frontend coherence, and UI/UX (Project memory: `project_audit_v2_complete`). The 65-item punch list distilled from those sweeps landed in twelve PRs, numbered #44 through #55, with code-quality and spec reviews per tier. Every numbered item is either code-fixed or formally documented as deferred with reasoning. The punch list itself stays in the repo at `docs/MARKET_READY_PUNCHLIST_V2.md` as the historical record. That document plus the PR ledger #44–#55 plus the new `tests/Feature/Audit/` test directory are the regression bar, encoded in CI so the wave cannot quietly regress.

**973 tests, 3,405 assertions, 100% passing.** Chapter 42 documents the test harness in detail. `php artisan test` against the current `main` runs 182 feature-test files (17,269 LOC of test code) producing 973 individual tests with 3,405 assertions. There are no skipped tests, no incomplete tests, no `@group skip`. The CI pipeline runs the full suite on every push. The test surface covers controllers, policies, services, events-and-listeners, queued jobs (synchronous in test), Inertia render shapes, FormRequest validation, and the statutory-correctness paths that are easy to get subtly wrong (PAYE bands, SSNIT MIE cap, Tier-2 trustee allocation, leave-balance race conditions, dual-control rejections).

**The canonical pattern enforced repo-wide.** Chapter 37 names the architecture pattern in one line: Enum → FormRequest → Service → Event → Listener → Resource → Inertia Page. Every module in the build follows it. New code that does not follow it is rejected at review. This is not a style preference — the pattern is what makes the test surface tractable (every layer has a single concern that can be tested in isolation) and what makes a new joiner productive in days rather than weeks. The pattern is walked module by module in Part II of the dossier and is the reason Chapter 38's data model and Chapter 39's RBAC tree have the regularity they do.

**SequenceService for every reference.** Chapter 37 also names the second canonical pattern: every human-facing reference number (`PAY-2026-0001`, `LOAN-2026-00042`, `OFFB-2026-0007`, `DSR-2026-0003`, document `ref_no`, etc.) is minted via `SequenceService::next()`, never via `count() + 1`. PR #21 closed the last `count()+1` site in Finance after a race-condition demonstration; the convention has been enforced repo-wide since. This is the kind of thing a buyer's auditor checks for; it is the kind of thing that, if missing, lets two payroll runs share `PAY-2026-0042` and the AG team writes a finding.

**The password-must-change wall on first login (PR #34).** Every admin-created user lands on a forced password-change screen on first login. Seeded fixed accounts ship with `password_must_change = true`. The flow is enforced in middleware, tested in the Audit tier, and applies equally to the `super_admin` seed and to the new joiner created from HR three minutes ago. Chapter 40's security walk-through documents the middleware path.

**TOTP 2FA shipped, gated where it matters.** Chapter 40 also documents the 2FA implementation. Standard TOTP via authenticator apps, backup recovery codes, and — critically — a `2fa:fresh` middleware variant that requires the user to have produced a TOTP within the last few minutes before they can hit a destructive endpoint. Every endpoint that approves payroll, completes off-boarding, posts a journal, dispatches a disbursement batch, fulfils a DSR erasure, triages a whistleblower report, or marks a payment paid is behind `2fa:fresh`. The gate is consistent across all those modules because it is one middleware, not seven copies.

**Tamper-evident SHA-256 audit chain — shipped.** This is the biggest single thing in the build, and the gap analysis (memory: `project_government_gap_analysis`) had it as Phase 1 priority. It shipped in v1.0. Chapter 24 walks the mechanics; the brief version is: every mutating route writes a queued audit row, every audit row carries a SHA-256 hash of canonical-JSON of the prior row plus its own payload, the chain is verifiable by an artisan command, and the verifier runs nightly with `--notify`. The Auditor-General Report Pack reads the chain and emits a verification artefact alongside the data exports. A buyer evaluating "can your audit trail survive a forensic review" gets a one-word answer.

**CEO role mirrors super_admin (PR #38).** Chapter 39 documents the RBAC tree; the executive-visibility decision was that the CEO role carries the wildcard permission set, so a CEO seat does not need to be granted module-by-module on every new release. PR #38 made this explicit; role-string checks now consistently match `'super_admin'`, `'ceo'`, and `'hr_admin'` together where executive read is intended. This is a small change with a large consequence — a board-level role does not silently lose access when a new module ships.

The aggregate: 973 tests, a punch list of 65 closed items in twelve auditable PRs, two canonical patterns enforced repo-wide, two security walls (password change, 2FA fresh) on every destructive path, and a tamper-evident audit chain in production. A buyer's IT director walks this with their checklist and finds the answers.

## Claim 3 — Finance and statutory compliance are the moat

This is the claim that distinguishes CIHRMS from "any HRMS with a Ghana skin". Most HRMS competitors in the mid-market — including the ones with strong Ghana sales — do not carry their own general ledger. They emit a payroll JV and hand it to whatever finance system the customer happens to run. CIHRMS does carry its own ledger, and the ledger is the same ledger that the payroll engine posts into. That is what makes the Finance Hub in Chapter 20 a live cash-position screen and not a stale dashboard.

**F1 through F5 fully shipped.** Chapter 20 walks the five-layer Finance build: F1 Chart of Accounts + Org Bank Accounts + Finance Hub, F2 Vendors + Accounts Payable + Journal Engine, F3 Customers + Accounts Receivable + Customer Statements with aging, F4 Paystack hosted-checkout gateway with HMAC-SHA512 webhook signatures and bulk refunds, F5 Bank Reconciliation with CSV / OFX 2.x / MT940 SWIFT parsers and a three-tier matcher emitting bank-adjustment journals for fees and interest. The audit records F1–F5 in the "shipped" array with no partials on the headline features; the recorded partials (`ApPaymentService::void()` lock tightening, MT940 parser permissiveness, multi-currency hooks pending) are real and named but do not block customer onboarding.

**Double-entry invariants enforced at posting.** Chapter 20's hard contract is that `JournalPostingService` is the sole writer to `gl_account_balances`. Debits = credits is asserted at posting. Balance equals the natural sum of posted lines, which means any drift is structurally impossible — a balance row is not "the running total we updated"; it is a function of the posted lines. The source is reconstructable both ways: from a balance row you can list the posted journal lines that produced it; from a posted journal line you can verify the balance row it contributes to. This is the property a serious finance audit asks about. It is encoded in the service contract, not in a stored procedure or a nightly reconciliation job.

**GIFMIS exporter — shipped.** The government gap analysis (memory: `project_government_gap_analysis`) had `GifmisJournalExporter` as Phase 3 work. Chapter 19 ships it now. Approved payroll runs emit balanced sub-ledger JVs in the GIFMIS format, asserting debit = credit on the way out, with a per-MDA GL map injected via environment configuration. The exporter writes the JV with full statutory line breakdown — gross, PAYE Cr, SSNIT employee Cr, SSNIT employer Cr, NHIA Cr, Tier-2 Cr — so a Ministry's GIFMIS officer drops the file into their existing upload process unchanged. The "download-and-upload" model is what every GIFMIS customer uses today; live REST push is on the Phase 3 roadmap behind an `auto_mint_on_paid` flag that already exists in the codebase.

**IPPD exporter — shipped.** The gap analysis didn't even mention IPPD as in-scope; CIHRMS shipped it anyway because the public-sector buyer needs it. Chapter 19 emits the IPPD2 / IPPD3 CAGD format — pipe-delimited, pesewa-integer, one row per employee per period — that the Controller and Accountant-General's Department actually consumes. The audit records this as "met" against the `IPPD2/IPPD3 CAGD format` standard.

**Full statutory payroll — PAYE, SSNIT, NHIA, Tier-2 — all shipped.** The gap analysis named statutory-grade payroll as a Phase 1 priority. Chapter 19 ships it in v1.0. Five statutory return generators emit on every approved run: PAYE CSV in GRA format, SSNIT Tier-1 schedule, NHIA allocation, per-trustee Tier-2 schedule, GhIPSS bank file. Every one of those is callable from the run detail page, and every one has a Pest feature test that asserts the file shape and the totals. The audit records every relevant standards row as "met" — Income Tax Act 896 Sixth Schedule, Pensions Act 766 §3, NHIA Act 852 §29, SSNIT Act PNDCL 247, E-Levy Act 1075, PFM Act 921 §11.

**Very few HRMS competitors carry their own ledger.** Of the mid-market HRMS products that compete for Ghana sales, the count that ship a posting general ledger with enforced double-entry invariants, a Chart of Accounts the customer can edit, an AP and AR sub-ledger with dual control, a hosted-checkout gateway, and a bank reconciliation engine with three matchers is — generously — in the low single digits, and none of them are the names a procurement office knows. CIHRMS carries one, and Chapter 20 is the walk-through. That is the moat.

## What "ready" means here

**Ready for the first paying customer.** A pilot customer who signs in Q3 of this calendar year can run the full HR cycle — onboarding, leave, attendance, performance, payroll, off-boarding, document workflow, complaints, whistleblower, audit pack — against their actual staff data starting on day one of cutover. Chapters 3 through 35 walk what they get; Chapter 43 walks the deployment posture they get it on. The known gaps in Chapter 44 are listed against the modules they affect, and none of them block a pilot.

**Ready for the tender process.** A government tender or a donor-funded procurement evaluation reads roughly the same shape: ISO 30414 disclosures by topic, DPA Act 843 compliance evidence, statutory payroll completeness, audit-trail integrity, identity-verification adequacy, and a list of references against named standards. Every chapter in Part I closes with a "Standards touchpoints" section. The aggregate of those sections is a tender response. Chapter 24's Auditor-General Report Pack is the artefact that closes most of the "show me the evidence" sub-questions.

**Ready to be evaluated by the buyer's IT.** A buyer's IT shop runs an architecture review, a security review, and an operational-readiness review. Chapter 36 walks the architecture; Chapter 40 walks security (password-must-change wall, 2FA, `2fa:fresh` middleware, signed-URL downloads, queued audit writes, RBAC depth); Chapter 41 walks performance posture; Chapter 42 walks the test surface; Chapter 43 walks deployment. None of those chapters claims more than the build delivers. A buyer's IT director can walk the dossier with their checklist and the answers will be in the same chapter the question is asked in.

## What "ready" does NOT mean

**Not feature-complete for a Ministry-wide rollout.** Chapter 44 lists the gaps that matter for that scale — live NIA verification (currently a manual-upload provider as default driver per Chapter 25), an in-system content layer for L&D (Chapter 7's Phase 3 content layer), department-scoped audience targeting on Announcements beyond role-only (Chapter 17), a realtime channel for Notifications and Chat beyond the polling shipped today (Chapters 14 and 16), and per-grade leave-entitlement schedules from the establishment table (Chapter 4 Phase 2). Chapter 46 sequences these in three phases with effort and dependency labels. A Ministry-wide rollout sits at the end of Phase 1, not at v1.0.

**Not production-hardened at the scale of the largest target buyer.** The MVP runs on Laravel queues with database-driven jobs and SQLite-in-dev / Postgres-in-prod with single-instance defaults. A six-thousand-employee Ministry rollout wants the Postgres migration baseline that Chapter 43 already specifies, Horizon for queue management, Redis for cache and session, and the operational telemetry that lives in Phase 1 of Chapter 46's roadmap. The build is engineered to take those upgrades; it has not yet been load-tested at the high end. The first pilot — a single mid-market customer or a single MDA — does not need any of that, and the upgrade path is not architectural rework.

**Not third-party penetration-tested or CSA-registered.** A Cybersecurity Authority registration under Act 1038 is a six-to-twelve-month project that begins with a third-party penetration test, a documented incident-response plan, and a board-level information-security policy. CIHRMS has none of those *yet*, and they are scheduled as Phase 4 of Chapter 46. A pilot customer who needs a CSA-registered product is not the right first customer; the rest of the pilot universe — commercial buyers, donor programmes, and pre-CSA-mandate MDAs — has no blocker here.
