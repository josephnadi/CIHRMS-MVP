# Chapter 30 — Departments

> *In one paragraph.* Departments is the organisational chart of the institute — every operating unit the workforce hangs off, with a unique code, a name, and (when set) a head. Every Employee row in Chapter 3 belongs to exactly one. Establishment positions in Chapter 8 roll up to one. Leave approvals in Chapter 4 route up through one. Payroll cost in Chapter 19 pivots on one. Reports in Chapter 31 group by one. In the MVP the structure is **flat** — there is no parent/child tree, no nested org chart — because every dashboard, RBAC scope, and aggregate query in the system works against a single `department_id` foreign key and a hierarchy would change the shape of every one of them. The Phase 1 establishment work (Ch 8) already gives us the org-chart shape via `positions.reports_to_position_id`; departments stay flat by design.

## Where to find it

- **Sidebar location:** there are two distinct entry points and they mean different things. The **Workforce** group → **Employees** page exposes a "Departments" button in its header that links to `/departments` — the **admin register** documented in this chapter, where HR creates, edits, and deletes departments. Separately, the sidebar has a **Departments** expandable group (Settings → Departments) that lists eight **department portals** (IT, HR, Marketing, Finance, Membership, PCP, CPD, Administration) — one Vue page per slug, each gated by its own `portal.*` permission. The two are *not* the same screen: the admin register is for shape-of-the-org work; the portal page is the dashboard the department itself looks at every morning.
- **Roles that see it:**
    - **super_admin** and **ceo** — every department, every action, including delete.
    - **hr_admin** — `employees.manage` is the load-bearing permission; HR creates, edits, and deletes departments and sees the headcount tile for every one.
    - **dept_head** — `employees.view` lets them see the register read-only via the policy's `viewAny`/`view`; for the department(s) they head, `User::managesDepartment($id)` flips `update` true so they can rename or re-describe their own unit. They do *not* get `create` or `destroy` — only HR adds or removes departments.
    - **manager** — no register access in the MVP. They see their own department on the Employees directory filter dropdown; they do not reach `/departments`.
    - **employee** — no register access. They see their own department label on the Profile portal (Ch 16).
    - **finance_officer / auditor / it_support** — no register access by default. Portal access is granted by the orthogonal `portal.<slug>` permissions — a finance officer typically holds `portal.finance` and lands on the Finance portal directly; an IT staffer holds `portal.it`.
- **Related modules:** Employees (Ch 3) — every employee carries a `department_id` and the Employees directory's "All Departments" dropdown is keyed off this register; Establishment (Ch 8) — every Position rolls up to one department and the `(department, grade, fiscal_year)` ceiling triple is keyed on it; Leave (Ch 4) — approval routing climbs to the department head when an employee's direct manager is unset; Payroll (Ch 19) — pay registers and GIFMIS exports group cost by department; Reports (Ch 31) — every workforce, leave, and payroll report has a "by department" pivot; RBAC (Ch 28) — the `user_roles.department_id` column scopes a role to a single department, which is how a manager becomes a *dept_head over Finance* without holding institute-wide HR permissions.

The card gradient pool (six cool-family gradients) is keyed by `dept.id % 6`, so a department's accent colour is stable across renders without the database carrying a `color` column.

## The data behind it

The departments register is the simplest table in the schema — a single row per operating unit:

- **`departments`** — `id`, `name` (string, unique), `code` (string, unique), `description` (text, nullable), `head_user_id` (foreign key to `users.id`, nullable, `nullOnDelete`), `deleted_at` (soft-delete timestamp), and the standard `created_at` / `updated_at` audit columns. The table was created on 2026-05-12 with the first three fields, gained `deleted_at` from the all-tables soft-deletes migration on 2026-05-13, and gained `head_user_id` from the RBAC migration on 2026-05-15. There is **no `parent_id`** — the structure is intentionally flat.

The relationships that hang off it:

- **`employees.department_id`** — one Employee belongs to one Department. Deleting a department is blocked while any active employee is still assigned to it (the `DomainException` pattern documented in Ch 3); the count of active employees is also rendered on every card so the UI can warn before the call.
- **`departments.head_user_id` → `users.id`** — one department has at most one head. The relationship is exposed on the User side as `User::headedDepartments()` (one user can head zero or many departments — concurrent heads are rare but legal) and on the Department side as `Department::head()`. `head_user_id` is nullable, so a department without a head is a legitimate state (typically during a transition).
- **`user_roles.department_id`** — the RBAC pivot can scope a role to a department. A user with a `dept_head` role pivot carrying `department_id = 5` is a department head over department 5 *via the RBAC pivot* even if they are not the `head_user_id` on the row. Both pathways feed `User::managedDepartmentIds()` and either one is sufficient for `User::managesDepartment(5)` to return true.
- **`positions.department_id`** — every approved post rolls up to one department (Ch 8). Deleting a department is also blocked by the establishment side: the position service refuses to leave orphaned posts behind.
- **`establishment_ceilings.department_id`** — the `(department, grade, fiscal_year)` ceiling triple is keyed on the department row. Deleting a department orphans the ceiling rows; the soft-delete approach (rather than a hard delete) is what keeps the ceiling history intact for audit.

What every reader of the screen needs to keep in mind:

- **The structure is flat.** There is no parent department, no nested hierarchy, no recursive tree. The Phase 1 establishment module gives us *position-level* hierarchy via `positions.reports_to_position_id` (Ch 8); the department layer above is deliberately one level deep. An institute that needs a divisional layer above departments models it on the position tree, not by adding a `parent_id` here. The reason is uniform — every dashboard, every RBAC scope, and every aggregate query in the system reads `department_id` as a single FK. Adding `parent_id` would change the shape of every one of those queries (recursive descendants, transitive RBAC, cumulative roll-ups), and the cost-vs-benefit has consistently come out against the change.
- **`head_user_id` is the operational owner; RBAC `user_roles.department_id` is the scoping mechanism.** The two overlap but they are not the same field. Setting `head_user_id` makes the user the headcount-display owner on the org chart and gives them `update` on the Department row through the policy; attaching a `dept_head` role pivot scoped to the department gives them the *operational permission set* (leave approvals, the dept-head dashboard, scoped employee visibility). Most institutes do both, and the RBAC seeder sets both when a head is appointed via the HR flow. They are kept separate so that a finance officer scoped to the Finance department for *payroll* reasons does not accidentally inherit "head of Finance" semantics on the org chart.
- **Code is the load-bearing identifier.** GIFMIS exports, audit findings, the static-portal slug map, and every cross-module foreign-key trace use the department code (not the id, not the name). Renaming a department in the UI is fine — the code stays. Changing the code is a multi-system migration and the MVP UI does not warn about it; Phase 1 adds a "Code change → audit cascade" sheet that lists every downstream system that needs updating.
- **Delete is a soft delete.** The `SoftDeletes` trait on the model means `DELETE /departments/{id}` writes `deleted_at` and removes the row from every list, but does not erase it. A deleted department can be restored from the console if needed; the audit trail of "who worked there in 2026" remains queryable. The "Cannot delete department: N employee(s) still assigned" flash error is the *first* line of defence; the soft delete itself is the second.

## How it talks to other modules

- **Employees (Chapter 3)** — every employee carries a `department_id` FK. The Employees directory's "All Departments" filter dropdown is populated by `EmployeeService::listDepartments()` — the same call the register itself uses, so the two are always in sync. `Employee::scopeVisibleTo($user)` filters by `whereIn('department_id', $user->managedDepartmentIds())` for any non-HR viewer, which is how the same query returns "everyone" to HR and "my team" to a department head without a separate code path.
- **Establishment (Chapter 8)** — every Position rolls up to one Department via `positions.department_id`; the `(department, grade, fiscal_year)` headcount ceiling is keyed on the department row. Deleting a department is blocked while any non-soft-deleted position is still assigned to it (the same `DomainException` pattern Employees uses). Phase 1's "approved vs filled" department-dashboard tile pulls its numbers by joining `positions` against `position_assignments` filtered to the department.
- **Leave (Chapter 4)** — leave approval routing climbs from the employee → manager → department head. When `Employee::manager_id` is null, `LeaveRequestService::approverFor()` falls back to `Department::head()` so the request still has an approver. The notification template `LeaveRequestPending` carries the department name in the email subject for context.
- **Payroll (Chapter 19)** — pay registers, payslip cohorts, and GIFMIS exports all group cost lines by `employees.department_id`. The payroll dashboard's "by department" stack is a direct `groupBy` on the same field. A department that has been soft-deleted but still has historical pay rows continues to surface in payroll exports for historical periods — that is intentional; restating "who worked in Finance in March 2026" must remain answerable even after Finance is renamed or retired.
- **Reports (Chapter 31)** — every workforce, leave, and payroll report has a "by department" pivot, driven by the same department register. The Auditor-General pack (Ch 24) groups establishment cost by department × funding source × grade, so an unbalanced (department, grade) ceiling is visible at a glance.
- **RBAC (Chapter 28)** — `user_roles.department_id` scopes a role pivot to a single department. A user with the `dept_head` legacy role and a `user_roles` row with `department_id = 5` is a department head over department 5; they get `employees.view` scoped to their team, `leave.approve` scoped to their team's requests, and `tickets.manage` scoped to their team's tickets. The same user without the pivot (just the legacy role) has *no* department scope and *no* permissions — the pivot is the load-bearing record.
- **Department portals (this chapter)** — the eight slug-keyed portals (`/departments/portal/{it,hr,marketing,finance,membership,pcp,cpd,administration}`) render from `Departments/Show.vue` and each gate on a `portal.<slug>` permission. Real Department records are looked up by code when the slug matches the codeMap; the portal currently displays only the static narrative, but the wiring is there for Phase 1 to pull live KPIs from the matching backend module.
- **Profile Portal (Chapter 16)** — the employee's department label is read straight from this register through the `Employee::department` belongs-to relation; no caching, so a department rename surfaces on the profile page on next render.

## Standards touchpoints

- **ISO 30414 §4.7 workforce planning** — the standard recommends disclosing workforce composition by organisational unit as the foundation for headcount planning. The Departments register is the table that makes this disclosure possible; the active-staff count on every card is the simplest cut of it, the Reports module (Ch 31) collects the same field across leave, payroll, and recruitment cuts. See Chapter 44.
- **ISO 30414 §4.6 organisational culture** — the standard's culture metrics (engagement, turnover, manager span-of-control) are all pivoted on the unit, which in CIHRMS terms is the department row. The portal page is the surfacing layer for the per-department cuts; the Phase 1 portal-KPI wiring lands the live numbers behind the placeholder values shipped today.
- **Public Services Commission (PSC) establishment control** — the PSC's approval framework operates on (department, grade, fiscal year) triples. The Departments register is one third of that triple; the Grades and Establishment Ceilings tables in Ch 8 supply the other two. Deleting a department mid-fiscal-year is therefore not a casual rename — it requires the matching ceiling rows to be archived and re-issued, which is why the UI uses soft delete and the establishment migration does *not* cascade department deletes onto ceilings.
- **Data Protection Act, 2012 (Act 843) §17 — lawful basis for processing personal data** — the Departments register itself processes no personal data (the head FK is an indirect reference, and the row carries only operational metadata). It is in scope of the DPA only as part of the joined-employee view; the same lawful-basis-of-employment-contract that covers the Employees table covers the FK on this side. See Chapter 26.
- **Auditor-General audit trail** — every department create, edit, soft-delete, and head-change writes to the audit log via the `audit` route middleware that wraps the whole department route group. The Phase 1 tamper-evident chain (Ch 44) extends the existing log entry into the hash-linked record the Auditor-General pack reads off.

## What's planned next

Three pieces of work are scheduled directly against this chapter for the Phase 1 UI follow-up (W1.30 series):

1. **Reassign-head picker and "Currently head: …" surface.** The slide-panel form gains a `head_user_id` dropdown scoped to users whose primary role is `manager`, `dept_head`, or `hr_admin`. The card body gains a small "Head: {name}" row underneath the description (or an "Unassigned" italic when null). The matching FormRequest (`StoreDepartmentRequest` and a new `UpdateDepartmentRequest`) is widened to accept and validate `head_user_id`. The RBAC engine already reads the column correctly via `User::headedDepartments()` and `User::managesDepartment()`; only the UI and the FormRequest changes are required to make the column user-visible.

2. **FormRequest consolidation.** The update path today validates inline in the controller rather than via a dedicated FormRequest, and the rules diverge from create on `code` (required vs nullable) and `description` (1000 vs 500). W1.30 consolidates into one `UpdateDepartmentRequest` that mirrors `StoreDepartmentRequest`'s rules with `Rule::unique('departments', 'name')->ignore($department->id)` and the equivalent on `code`, so a rename hits the per-field validation error rather than the DB integrity error.

3. **Portal KPI wiring.** Each of the eight portals currently shows hard-coded KPI values from the `PORTALS` constant in `Departments/Show.vue`. The Phase 1 work moves the values to a stats endpoint per portal — IT pulls from the Tickets module (Ch 11), HR pulls from the Employees module (Ch 3), Finance pulls from the Payroll Engine (Ch 19), and so on. The portal's `StaticPageController::department` action gains a `stats` array in the Inertia payload, the Vue template reads it instead of the constant, and the portal page becomes a real-time dashboard rather than a styled skeleton.

A fourth piece — a *department detail* page (`/departments/{id}`) that lists the head, the active position roster, the establishment ceiling, and the employee count broken down by grade — is on the Phase 2 backlog and depends on the establishment module being wired to the department register through `positions.department_id` (already in place, see Ch 8).
