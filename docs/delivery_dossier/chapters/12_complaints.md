# Chapter 12 — Complaints

> *In one paragraph.* Complaints is the **named, lower-severity grievance channel** that sits inside the day-to-day HR estate: an employee or department head opens a written grievance about a workplace issue — an unfair shift, an interpersonal dispute, a missed allowance, an environment concern — and HR triages it, assigns an investigator, runs it through four documented states (Open → Under Review → Resolved → Closed), and closes the loop. Every complaint is stamped with an auto-generated `CMP-XXXXXXXX` reference at intake, and that reference is the submitter's lookup key on the public `/complaints/track` page — they get back a status pill and two timestamps, no PII. The whole module shares the application's standard authenticated chrome (sidebar, masthead, RBAC tree) — deliberately so, because Complaints is *the* HR-managed channel for ordinary workplace issues. It is **explicitly distinct from Whistleblower (Chapter 27)** at the schema, permission, policy, and routing layers — the two modules do not share a table, do not share a permission tree, and do not share an investigator pool. Whistleblower is for the protected disclosures (corruption, fraud, illegality) that by law must bypass HR; Complaints is everything else. The MVP ships the bare bones of this channel: intake, status board, inline reassign, public track-by-reference, and a slide-panel detail view; the deeper comment thread, the resolution-summary surface, and the per-status notification fan-out are roadmapped.

## Where to find it

- **Sidebar location:** Complaints is **not a top-level sidebar item** in the MVP layout. The page lives at `/complaints` under the page title **"Governance — Complaints"** with a masthead label of **"COMPLAINTS GAZETTE · ACT 715"** in the cobalt-on-cream "Editorial-Sovereign" style. The route is reached through three paths: (1) the **Department Head dashboard** (`RoleDeptHead.vue`) carries a "Complaints" quick-action tile in its action grid; (2) any role with `complaints.create` can hit `/complaints` directly and is dropped onto the **Submit Complaint** tab; (3) any role with `complaints.manage` lands on the **Management Queue** tab by default. The intentional absence from the sidebar is a soft signal — Complaints is supposed to feel like a form you fill in, not a system you operate. The Whistleblower lane, by contrast, *does* get a top-level sidebar slot under "System" because its surface is operated by a designated investigator team.
- **Public track endpoint:** `GET /complaints/track?reference=CMP-XXXXXXXX` is the one route in this module that sits **outside the `auth` middleware** — it is mounted at the top of `routes/web.php` alongside the kiosk and whistleblower public routes. A submitter who left the organisation, or who never had an account in the first place (the form accepts anyone with `complaints.create`), can still come back with their reference and look up the status pill and the last-updated timestamp.
- **Roles that see it:**
    - **super_admin** and **ceo** — wildcard `*` permission, see every complaint in the management queue.
    - **hr_admin** — explicitly seeded `complaints.create` + `complaints.manage`. The default investigator profile.
    - **manager** and **dept_head** — explicitly seeded `complaints.create` only. They can file, they cannot manage. The dept-head dashboard surfaces an "Open complaints" KPI as an *org-wide* indicator (see the note from `DashboardService.php` below) — the dept-head sees the institute's count, not their team's, because complaints carry no `employee_id` FK by design.
    - **employee, finance_officer, it_support, auditor** — all explicitly seeded `complaints.create`. Anyone in the organisation can file; very few can triage. This is the deliberate slope of the channel.
    - **No role-level shadow** — there is no special "complaints office" role in the MVP. The triage pool is "anyone with `complaints.manage`", which today maps to hr_admin + super_admin + ceo. The Phase 1 work to widen the pool is roadmapped.
- **Related modules:** **Whistleblower (Ch 27)** — the sibling escalation lane for the disclosures that must not flow through HR; the two channels live on different tables, different policies, different permission trees. **Service Desk / Tickets (Ch 11 in the legacy numbering — Tasks)** — *service* issues (a broken printer, a slow VPN, a forgotten password reset) are tickets; *HR* issues (an unfair allocation, a disputed shift, an interpersonal grievance) are complaints. **Notifications (Ch 16)** — status-change fan-out and submitter acknowledgement are roadmapped; the MVP fires nothing. **Audit Logs (Ch 24)** — every authenticated mutation on the complaint runs through the standard Laravel audit chain. **Standards (Ch 44)** — ISO 10002, Labour Act 651 §65, and DPA Act 843 §39 touchpoints land here.

A burst-protection throttle is on the roadmap nonetheless.

## The data behind it

CIHRMS stores a complaint in a **single table — `complaints`** — with no satellite tables, no thread table, no actions table, no evidence table, no subjects table. The intentional contrast with Whistleblower (which carries five tables) is one of severity proportionality: a workplace grievance does not warrant the segregated case-file infrastructure that a protected disclosure under Act 720 does.

The columns on the `complaints` row:

- **`id`** — primary key.
- **`reference`** — unique `CMP-XXXXXXXX` string generated by `ComplaintService::generateReference()` using `strtoupper(Str::random(8))`. The service loops until a non-collision is found — a probabilistic uniqueness check (32^8 ≈ 1.1 trillion combinations) backed by a DB unique index as the hard floor. The 12-character total length is asserted in the test suite (`expect(strlen($complaint->reference))->toBe(12)`).
- **`submitted_by`** — `string`, defaults to `'anonymous'`. **This is a free text column, not a foreign key to `users`.** Even if a logged-in employee files the complaint, the service writes the typed name (or `'anonymous'`) — there is no shadow column that links the row to a User id. This was a deliberate choice (see the comment in `DashboardService.php:395`: *"complaints have no employee_id FK by design (anonymous-friendly, submitted_by is a free string per Whistleblower Act)"*).
- **`assigned_to`** — nullable foreign key to `users.id`, `nullOnDelete()`. Added in a follow-up migration (`2026_05_17_055805_add_assigned_to_to_complaints.php`). Indexed for register queries that filter or scope by assignee. The `Complaint::assignee()` relation lazy-loads the user (id + name only) for the resource.
- **`details`** — `text`, the body of the grievance. Stored as plaintext (not encrypted), reflecting the lower-severity stance — by contrast, Whistleblower's `description` is `encrypted` at rest.
- **`status`** — `string`, defaults to `'open'`. Cast to the `ComplaintStatus` enum on read; only the four enum cases (`open`, `under_review`, `resolved`, `closed`) survive a write because the FormRequest gates the input through `Rule::enum(ComplaintStatus::class)`.
- **`created_at`, `updated_at`** — standard Eloquent timestamps. The "Submitted" date on every surface is `created_at`; the "Last Updated" date on the track card is `updated_at`.
- **`deleted_at`** — soft delete enabled via the `SoftDeletes` trait. Operationally, a deleted complaint is removed from every list but kept in the DB for recoverability.

What every reader of the screen needs to keep in mind:

- **Complaints are named-but-optional, not anonymous-by-default.** The text on the form ("Leave blank to remain anonymous") makes the choice explicit, and the default value when blank is the literal string `'anonymous'`. Compare to Whistleblower where the anonymity toggle defaults to ON, the row has *no* `submitted_by` column at all, and a tracking-code hash is the only re-entry handle. The two channels are deliberately opposite defaults.
- **There is no submitter-identity column for follow-up.** Even named complaints carry only the typed string. If a submitter writes "Akua M.", that is what the investigator sees — there is no email field, no phone field, no `submitter_user_id`. A submitter who wants to be called back has to put their contact in the `details` body, which is acceptable in the MVP context but is a usability gap relative to Whistleblower's dedicated encrypted `submitter_contact` field. The richer contact intake is on the roadmap.
- **The reference is a server-generated random string, not a hashed token.** A submitter who knows another submitter's reference can look up status (the public track endpoint will happily render the resource). They cannot see the body of the complaint — the public surface only shows reference, status, and two timestamps — but they can see the case exists and what stage it is at. For a low-severity workplace grievance channel this is an acceptable surface; for a protected disclosure channel it would not be, which is precisely why Whistleblower uses a SHA-256-hashed tracking code with constant-work lookup.
- **The body is plaintext at rest.** `complaints.details` is a plain `text` column, no `encrypted` cast. This matches the channel's severity model (DPA Act 843 §17 lawful-basis processing rather than §39 sensitive-data treatment). When a complaint contains sensitive-personal-data content — e.g. an allegation of harassment — it should be escalated to Whistleblower, which carries the encrypted-at-rest envelope. The escalation pathway itself is not yet wired (see "Honest gaps").
- **Soft delete is recovery, not erasure.** A deleted complaint stays in the DB. DPA Act 843 §40 erasure requests are handled by the same separate redaction job that covers Employees and every other module.

## How it talks to other modules

- **No event is fired.** Unlike Employee (which fires `EmployeeCreated`) and Whistleblower (which fires `WhistleblowerReportSubmitted` and `WhistleblowerCaseClosed`), the Complaints submission service emits nothing. There is no `ComplaintSubmitted`, no `ComplaintStatusChanged`, no listener wiring. The Notifications module (Ch 16) has no hook to fan-out a status-change email or in-app banner — and so today, when a complaint goes from Open to Resolved, the submitter is not told. The only way they learn is by hitting the public track endpoint with their reference. This is the **single largest functional gap** on the module and is item one on the Phase 2 follow-up list.
- **No notification class exists.** A `grep` over `app/Notifications` for "complaint" returns no files. There is no `ComplaintAcknowledged` mailable, no `ComplaintStatusUpdated` in-app notification. The fan-out surface is empty.
- **Dashboard (Ch 13)** — `DashboardService::index()` exposes `openComplaints` as `Complaint::open()->count()` on the top-level snapshot, which the CEO/admin overview tile renders. `DashboardService::getDeptHeadSnapshot()` also exposes `open_complaints`, but **org-wide**, not scoped to the department head's team — the inline comment explains why: complaints carry no `employee_id` FK, so the per-team scope cannot be computed. The dept-head dashboard surfaces the figure as a general indicator.
- **Audit Logs (Ch 24)** — every authenticated mutation runs through the standard Laravel audit chain. The public `/complaints/track` GET is read-only and does not emit; the public-submission case for Whistleblower (which does write but deliberately bypasses the chain to avoid creating an identity trail) does not apply here because Complaints submissions require `auth + complaints.create`, so the user identity is already in the system.
- **Whistleblower (Ch 27) — explicit distinction in code, verified.** Different table (`complaints` vs `whistleblower_reports`), different controller (`ComplaintController` vs `WhistleblowerController` + admin counterpart), different service (`ComplaintService` vs `WhistleblowerSubmissionService` + `WhistleblowerInvestigationService`), different policy posture (Complaints relies on permission middleware only — there is no `ComplaintPolicy` class; Whistleblower carries a dedicated `WhistleblowerReportPolicy`), different permission tree (`complaints.create` + `complaints.manage` vs `whistleblower.investigate` + `whistleblower.view_all` + `whistleblower.manage`), different role seeding (hr_admin holds `complaints.manage` but is *deliberately not granted any whistleblower permission* by the seeder — the audit trail of permission seeding records this absence), different anonymity model (named-but-optional with a free-string column vs anonymous-by-default with a hashed tracking code), different encryption posture (plaintext vs `encrypted` cast on every sensitive column), different intake surface (authenticated form inside the app vs un-authenticated `/whistleblower` page with no app chrome), and different referral semantics (Complaints closes with `Resolved` / `Closed`; Whistleblower closes with `closed_substantiated` / `closed_unsubstantiated` / `closed_referred` to CHRAJ / Auditor-General / Police). The two modules are not just logically distinct — they share **no table, no controller, no service, no resource, no request, no model, no event, no policy, no enum.** A grep for `Complaint` in the `app/Models` directory returns one file; a grep for `Whistleblower` returns six. The separation is genuine.
- **Service Desk / Tickets (Ch 11)** — adjacent but distinct: a *service* problem (broken laptop, slow VPN, blocked SSO login) goes through Tickets and is owned by IT; an *HR* grievance (unfair shift, interpersonal dispute, missed allowance) goes through Complaints and is owned by HR. The two share neither table nor permission tree (`tickets.create` / `tickets.manage` are entirely separate slugs from `complaints.create` / `complaints.manage`), and both surface on the dept-head dashboard as separate KPIs.
- **Sidebar (Layouts/AuthenticatedLayout.vue)** — Complaints is *not* a sidebar item. Discoverability runs through the dept-head dashboard tile and direct URL navigation. The deliberate sidebar absence is a soft signal that this is a form-you-fill, not a system-you-operate.

## Standards touchpoints

- **ISO 10002 — Customer satisfaction · Guidelines for complaints handling in organisations.** ISO 10002 specifies that a complaints process should be **visible, accessible, responsive, charged-no-fees, confidential, and provide closing-the-loop notification**. The MVP delivers four of the six: visibility (the queue exists, the form is discoverable from the dept-head dashboard), accessibility (the form takes ≤ 60 seconds to submit, plain English throughout), no-fees (it is an internal channel), and confidentiality (the submitter can stay anonymous; the body is read only by `complaints.manage` holders). The two unmet: **responsiveness** (no SLA timer, no overdue banner) and **closing-the-loop notification** (no event, no mail, no in-app fan-out — the submitter only learns by polling the track endpoint). Both are on the roadmap. See Chapter 44.
- **Ghana Labour Act 2003 (Act 651) §64–§67 — grievance procedures.** Act 651 §64 obliges every employer with 25+ workers to establish a grievance procedure with documented steps and time limits. The CIHRMS Complaints module is the documented internal step — the form-of-record under the employer's procedure. **What is *not* yet captured:** the §65–§66 statutory time limits (the worker shall present the grievance "in the first instance, to the immediate superior" within a defined window, and so on through the escalation ladder) are not enforced anywhere in the schema or service today. Phase 2 work on the SLA timer and an explicit escalation-step field (`step_1_supervisor`, `step_2_dept_head`, `step_3_hr`, `step_4_chro`) is the operational expression of §65–§66. See Chapter 44.
- **Data Protection Act 2012 (Act 843) §17 — lawful basis for processing personal data.** Complaints content is processed under the employment-contract lawful basis (§17(c)) — same as Employee personal data. The body is stored plaintext because the channel is not handling §39 sensitive-personal-data categories by design; when a complaint *does* contain sensitive content (a harassment allegation, a disability matter), the operational instruction is to escalate to Whistleblower, which carries the §39-grade encrypted-at-rest envelope. The escalation pathway is not yet wired (see "Honest gaps"). See Chapter 26 and Chapter 44.
- **Data Protection Act §40 — retention & erasure.** Complaints use `SoftDeletes` and are retained indefinitely in the MVP. A defined retention schedule (typically: resolved → purge after N years; closed → shorter window) is on the Phase 2 roadmap, parallel to the same work on Whistleblower and Employee. See Chapter 26.
- **Public Services Commission (PSC) grievance policy — internal-resolution timelines.** The PSC HR Handbook §5.4 recommends an internal-resolution clock of 14 working days from filing to first response. The MVP does not yet measure, display, or enforce this clock. A SLA-banner on the queue header (red after 14 working days for any case still in `open`, amber within 3 working days of breach) is the parallel of Whistleblower's overdue-triage banner and is on the roadmap. See Chapter 44.
- **ISO 10002 §7 — operation of the complaints-handling process.** The standard prescribes acknowledgement-on-receipt, initial-assessment-and-classification, investigation, response, closure, and closing-the-loop notification. The MVP delivers acknowledgement (the green confirmation card carries the reference), initial assessment (manual — by reading the row in the queue), investigation (manual — the investigator reads the body and acts off-system), response (manual — the investigator calls or messages the submitter outside the system; no in-app message thread exists), closure (the Resolved / Closed status), and notification (only via submitter polling on the track endpoint). The first-class versions of every manual step are on the roadmap. See Chapter 44.

## What's planned next

The MVP shipped the bare-bones grievance channel; the gap analysis (eight days old at the time of writing) marked the module as a Phase 2 priority alongside Whistleblower hardening. Six concrete next steps are on the immediate roadmap:

1. **`ComplaintSubmitted` and `ComplaintStatusChanged` events + notification fan-out.** The single largest functional gap. Today a submitter who files a complaint receives a reference and is then silent until they next poll. Phase 2 fires `ComplaintSubmitted` at intake with a listener that emails the submitter (when a name has been given that resolves to a known user — the system would need a soft email-lookup helper for this) and posts an in-app banner to the investigator pool. `ComplaintStatusChanged` fires on every state change and emails the submitter when their case moves to Resolved / Closed. This is the ISO 10002 §7 closing-the-loop notification, made first-class.
2. **In-app message thread on the detail panel.** Today the detail panel is read-only. Phase 2 adds a `complaint_messages` satellite table (mirror of `whistleblower_messages` but without the encryption cast), a two-way thread on the detail panel, and a submitter-side reply form on the track surface. The submitter re-authenticates by re-supplying their reference, the same pattern Whistleblower uses for the tracking code.
3. **Resolution-summary surface and visible-to-submitter outcome notes.** Today the closure is just a status pill change. Phase 2 adds a `resolution_summary` text column on the row plus a "Closure summary" textarea in the panel; on closure the summary is rendered on the public track card so the submitter sees what was decided. This is the ISO 10002 §7 response step, made first-class.
4. **SLA timer + overdue banner under Labour Act §65–§66 / PSC 14-working-day clock.** A red banner appears on the queue header when any complaint is still in `open` after 14 working days; an amber banner appears 3 days before breach. Parallel to the existing Whistleblower SLA banner.
5. **Escalation-to-Whistleblower pathway.** When a triager reads a complaint and judges it falls under the §3 Act 720 protected-disclosure list, today they have to manually close the complaint and re-open the matter on `/whistleblower`. Phase 2 adds an "Escalate to Whistleblower" action on the panel that closes the complaint with `closed_escalated`, opens a new whistleblower case with the body copied across (encrypted-at-rest in the new envelope), and gives the submitter a fresh tracking code. The cross-link between the two cases is recorded for the investigator but not for the submitter.
6. **Throttle + abuse defence on the submission endpoint.** The MVP relies on "you must be logged in" as the abuse defence. Phase 2 adds a per-user `throttle:10,60` (10 per hour) plus an admin-side "block submitter" toggle for the edge case of a single user filing twenty duplicate complaints in an hour. The trade-off — making submission less frictionless to defend a tiny edge case — is the reason this is item six and not item one.

Each of these is small in code-size but consequential in user-experience surface; collectively they take the module from "grievance form-of-record" to "ISO 10002-compliant complaints management". The work is targeted for the same Phase 2 window as the Whistleblower hardening so the two sibling channels grow up together.
