# Chapter 26 ‚Äî Data Protection (Act 843) & Privacy

> *In one paragraph.* The Privacy module is CIHRMS's statutory answer to Ghana's Data Protection Act 2012 (Act 843). Any data subject ‚Äî whether they still log in, never logged in, or had their account purged years ago ‚Äî can file an Access, Rectification, Erasure, Portability, Objection, or Information request and CIHRM Ghana has thirty days under ¬ß22 to respond. The codebase carries the full lifecycle in two halves: a **public-facing DPA portal** at `/dpa` for people who don't (or no longer) have a login, gated by an emailed magic-link verification; and an **authenticated Privacy module** at `/privacy` for the people who do, surfaced from the Profile portal. Both feed the same `DPO Queue` at `/admin/privacy`, where the Auditor ‚Äî who doubles as the Data Protection Officer to keep HR out of erasure decisions about its own employees ‚Äî acknowledges, fulfils, or rejects each request with a written statutory basis. Fulfilment produces a tamper-evident ZIP export with a SHA-256 hash; erasure tombstones PII while preserving rows the law forces us to keep (payroll six years, SSNIT seven years, the audit chain forever). The whole lifecycle is itself audit-logged, both inside the `data_subject_requests` row's own `audit_trail` JSON and in the tamper-evident chain from Chapter 24.

## Where to find it

- **Sidebar location:**
    - **Admin** group ‚Üí **DPA Requests** (`policy` icon, visible only when `privacy.fulfill` is granted). This is the DPO's queue.
    - **There is no sidebar entry for the subject self-service page in the MVP.** Employees reach `/privacy` from the Profile portal (Chapter 16) or by direct URL; this is a known nav gap and is listed in *What's planned next* below.
- **Public portal:** `/dpa` is **unauthenticated** and reachable from the institute home page footer ("Privacy Charter" link), throttled to ten requests per minute per IP. It is the only mechanism for ex-employees, failed applicants, and members of the public to exercise their Act 843 rights.
- **Roles that see it:**
    - **super_admin** ‚Äî bypasses every policy check via `DataSubjectRequestPolicy::before()`; sees and can fulfil any request, and is the only role that can sign off on `privacy.erase` (the destructive tombstoning step).
    - **auditor** ‚Äî the default Data Protection Officer in the seeded role matrix. Holds `privacy.fulfill`, sees the entire DPO queue, can acknowledge, fulfil non-erasure requests, and reject with a statutory citation. The seeder explicitly notes that auditor (not HR) gets the lane "to avoid conflict of interest where HR processes their own employees' erasure requests".
    - **Every authenticated user** ‚Äî has the inherent right to file a request about themselves and to withdraw it before fulfilment; no permission is required. They never see anyone else's request.
    - **Anyone with an email address** ‚Äî can file via the public `/dpa` portal without ever having had a CIHRMS account, provided they verify their address via the emailed magic link.
- **Related modules:** Employees (Chapter 3) ‚Äî the data subject of every employment-related request, and the source for most of the export bundle; Audit Logs (Chapter 24) ‚Äî every transition on a DSR row appends a tamper-evident entry, and the `12-audit_log_entries.json` slice of the export pack draws from that chain; Identity (Chapter 25) ‚Äî Ghana Card verifications are listed in the export and tombstoned-around (kept for the seven-year SSNIT retention floor) on erasure; Profile Portal (Chapter 16) ‚Äî the in-app self-service entry point to `/privacy/my`; Whistleblower (separately) ‚Äî content from cases where the subject is a respondent is **explicitly excluded** from exports under Whistleblower Act 2006 (Act 720), and that exclusion is recorded on the manifest cover sheet.

## The data behind it

A `data_subject_requests` row is one piece of legal correspondence ‚Äî every column exists to answer a specific Act 843 question:

- **Identity of the subject.** Either `subject_user_id` (authenticated submission) **or** `subject_email` + `subject_full_name` + `verification_token` (public submission). The two paths cannot mix on the same row.
- **What they want.** `request_type` is one of six enum values (`access`, `rectification`, `erasure`, `portability`, `objection`, `information`) and `subject_statement` is the verbatim text. `rectification_details` and `objection_purpose` are the type-specific extras.
- **Where in the lifecycle.** `status` walks through ten possible values ‚Äî `pending_verification` (public-only, pre-magic-link), `submitted`, `acknowledged`, `in_review`, `pending_subject`, `fulfilled`, `partially_fulfilled`, `rejected`, `withdrawn`, `overdue` ‚Äî with `isTerminal()` carving out the last four as final.
- **Timeline.** `submitted_at`, `target_completion_date` (always submission + 30 days, reset on public verification), `acknowledged_at`, `completed_at`. `daysRemaining()` and `isOverdue()` are computed on the model so the API and the UI agree.
- **Who handled it.** `assigned_to` (the DPO once acknowledged), `decided_by` (the DPO who fulfilled or rejected).
- **What was decided.** `decision_summary` (visible to the subject, minimum twenty chars), `rejection_basis` (required on reject, minimum five chars, a statutory citation).
- **Tamper-evident artefacts.** `export_path` (filesystem path of the ZIP), `export_sha256` (the hash baked into the manifest), `export_generated_at`. The `tombstone_log` JSON, written only for Erasure fulfilments, lists every redacted field and every held-back row with the statute that kept it.
- **Per-request audit.** `audit_trail` is a JSON array appended via `DataSubjectRequest::appendAuditEntry()` on every state change ‚Äî `submitted`, `acknowledged`, `export_built`, `erasure_executed`, `fulfilled`, `rejected`, `withdrawn_by_subject`, `public_submitted`, `verified_by_token`. This is the per-row micro-audit; it complements the global tamper-evident chain (Chapter 24) and does not depend on it.

The reference number `DSR-YYYY-NNNNN` is allocated by `DataSubjectRequestService::nextReference()` ‚Äî a `count() + 1` per year (a known concurrency gap; the system-wide `SequenceService` pattern documented elsewhere in the audit pack is not yet wired here). Soft delete is enabled on the table but is not used by the lifecycle ‚Äî a withdrawn or rejected request stays visible with its terminal status; we never silently disappear correspondence.

What every reader of the queue needs to keep in mind:

- **Pending-verification rows are invisible to the queue.** `markOverdueRequests()` ignores them and so does the default DPO view, so spam from the public form never reaches the auditor.
- **The thirty-day clock starts at verification, not at submission, for public requests.** This is recorded explicitly in `verifyPublic()` ‚Äî the `submitted_at` column is rewritten on the verify step.
- **Authenticated submissions skip verification.** They start at `submitted` and the clock runs from `submitted_at` immediately, because identity is already established by the login session.
- **The verification token is hidden from the model.** It is in `$hidden`, so any JSON response (including the DPO's own admin show page) never carries it. Anyone with the token can flip a `pending_verification` row to `submitted` ‚Äî that is by design but it is also the only thing the token can do.
- **Erasure cannot delete what the law requires us to keep.** `ErasureService` tombstones identifying fields on the User and Employee rows, deletes employee skills and avatar paths outright, and counts every row still inside a statutory hold (six years for payroll under Income Tax Act 2015 ¬ß43, seven years for SSNIT under Pensions Act 2008 ¬ß92, indefinitely for the audit chain). The held-back rows still exist but no longer identify the data subject because the joining `users.name`, `users.email`, and `employees.*` columns have been stamped with `[ERASED YYYY-MM-DD ¬∑ DSR-XXXX-NNNNN]`.

## What an export looks like

A fulfilled Access, Portability, or Information request produces a ZIP at `storage/app/dsr-exports/{reference}.zip`. Inside:

- **`00-account.json`** ‚Äî the User row (id, name, email, staff_id, role, 2FA enrolment flag, WhatsApp phone, notification preferences, created_at).
- **`01-employee.json`** ‚Äî the Employee row (employee number, department, position, hire date, status, gender, DOB, Ghana Card PIN, SSNIT, TIN, address, phone, emergency contact block, bank block, mobile money block, salary, current grade, current step).
- **`02-leave_requests.json`** ‚Äî every leave request the employee filed.
- **`03-payroll_lines.json`** ‚Äî every payroll line, with period reference, basic, gross, PAYE, SSNIT tier 1, Tier 2, net, status.
- **`04-attendance.json`** ‚Äî every attendance summary row (date, status, first in, last out, hours worked).
- **`05-performance_reviews.json`** ‚Äî every performance review with cycle id, type, overall rating, status, submitted-at, and comments.
- **`06-loan_accounts.json`** ‚Äî every loan account.
- **`07-skills.json`** ‚Äî every skill row.
- **`08-identity_verifications.json`** ‚Äî every Ghana Card check (provider, status, verified-at, expires-at, plus the last character of the card number only).
- **`09-tickets_filed.json`** / **`10-complaints_filed.json`** / **`11-job_applications.json`** ‚Äî anything the subject submitted into the system.
- **`12-audit_log_entries.json`** ‚Äî the most-recent five thousand audit-log entries where the subject was the actor (capped to keep the export reasonable).
- **A CSV companion for every JSON** ‚Äî the same data flattened to comma-separated values so the subject can open it in Excel, Numbers, or LibreOffice without help.
- **`MANIFEST.md`** ‚Äî a Markdown cover sheet listing every file with a per-file SHA-256 hash and byte count. It also lists three categories of statutory exclusions: whistleblower content (Act 720 ¬ß10 anonymity supersedes Act 843), 2FA secrets and recovery codes (excluded because disclosing them would weaken the subject's own account security), and other users' data the subject may have viewed (because the export is data **about** the subject, not data the subject has seen).

The ZIP's own SHA-256 hash is computed in `DataSubjectExportBuilder::buildFor()` and stored on the `data_subject_requests` row, so anyone questioning the bundle three years later can re-hash the file and verify it byte-for-byte.

## Consent ‚Äî what's tracked, what's not

The MVP tracks consent in exactly one place: **WhatsApp messaging**. Meta requires explicit opt-in before a phone number can receive template messages, so `users.whatsapp_consent_at` exists as the timestamp of opt-in, paired with `users.whatsapp_phone` and a `notification_channels` JSON map keyed by channel slug. Slack, email, and in-app are governed by the same JSON map, but the implicit-consent posture for email (it is the employee's contractual contact channel) means there is no per-channel timestamp.

There is no general-purpose consent ledger. Performance-review participation, leave routing, payroll processing, biometric attendance, and Ghana Card verification all run on the **employment-contract lawful basis** under Act 843 ¬ß20(1)(b) ‚Äî consent is not required and is not tracked. This is the correct posture for an internal HRMS, but it is also why the system has no answer to "withdraw consent for X processing"; the closest workflow is an **Objection** request via `/privacy`, which the DPO then reviews against the contractual basis.

Cookie / browser-tracking consent is **not present** in the MVP. There is no analytics cookie banner, no tracking pixel, and no third-party advertising tag ‚Äî the app is a single-tenant institute deployment, not a public marketing site ‚Äî so the European-style cookie banner has no statutory hook to require it. This is documented here as a gap rather than a feature, because some institutes will want a consent banner when they deploy to a `.gov.gh` subdomain that also serves public content (the recruitment portal at `/careers`, the whistleblower portal at `/whistleblower`, the public DPA portal at `/dpa`).

## How it talks to other modules

- **`DataSubjectRequestSubmitted` event** ‚Üí fires on every authenticated submission and on every successful public verification. Today it has no listeners wired by default; the audit log captures the transition via the per-row `audit_trail`, and the DPO sees the row in their queue on next page load. Hook point for future notifications to the assigned DPO is in place.
- **`DataSubjectRequestFulfilled` event** ‚Üí fires on fulfilment with the fresh model attached. Same posture ‚Äî the row records the fulfilment in its own audit trail, and the event is available for downstream listeners (e.g. a future "your data is ready" notification).
- **Employees (Chapter 3)** ‚Üí the export builder reads every joined table (leave, payroll, attendance, performance, identity, skills) from the Employee record. Erasure tombstones rotate around the same row, redacting personal identifiers while keeping employment numbers needed for statutory retention.
- **Audit Logs (Chapter 24)** ‚Üí every state transition on a DSR row also writes an entry to the global tamper-evident chain via the standard `AuditTrail` middleware. The per-request `audit_trail` JSON is the local-view supplement; the chain is the legally significant ledger.
- **Identity (Chapter 25)** ‚Üí `IdentityVerification` rows for the subject's Ghana Card are listed in `08-identity_verifications.json` and are explicitly enumerated in the erasure held-back log under Pensions Act ¬ß92's seven-year retention.
- **Whistleblower (Act 720, separate chapter)** ‚Üí content where the subject is named as a respondent (not as the submitter) is excluded from the export and noted on the manifest. The exclusion is statutory, not configurable.
- **2FA (Chapter 25 / authentication chapter)** ‚Üí the fulfil action is behind `2fa:fresh`, requiring a fresh second-factor challenge within the rolling window used by `payroll.reverse`, `loans.disburse`, and `ar_invoices.write_off`. Erasure additionally requires the `privacy.erase` permission, which is held only by super_admin in the seeded matrix.
- **Profile portal (Chapter 16)** ‚Üí the in-app entry to `/privacy/my`. The portal is the only place a non-super_admin currently sees the link, because the sidebar entry exists only for the DPO admin view.

## Standards touchpoints

- **Data Protection Act, 2012 (Act 843) ¬ß¬ß17‚Äì22 ‚Äî data-subject rights.** Every enum case in `DataSubjectRequestType` maps to a numbered section: ¬ß17 access, ¬ß18 rectification, ¬ß19 erasure, ¬ß20 objection, ¬ß21 portability, ¬ß22 information. The thirty-day SLA constant in `DataSubjectRequestService::SLA_DAYS` is the ¬ß22 timeline. The "first request in any 12-month period is free of charge" text on the My Privacy page is the ¬ß24 fee posture. The rejection workflow requires a statutory citation in `rejection_basis`; the seeded example on the case-file UI is "Act 843 ¬ß27(e) ‚Äî public-interest archive", one of the named exemptions.
- **Data Protection Act ¬ß28 ‚Äî record of processing.** The `audit_trail` JSON on every DSR row is itself a record-of-processing artefact for the privacy module, supplementing the global audit chain.
- **Data Protection Act ¬ß39 ‚Äî sensitive personal data.** Ghana Card numbers, SSNIT numbers, TINs, biometric attendance records, and bank details are exported in full to the subject (data **about** them) but are masked to the last four digits everywhere else in the UI ‚Äî see Chapter 3's Ghana Compliance card and Chapter 25's biometric notes. Salary is gated behind `employees.view_salary` and is omitted from API responses entirely for users without the permission; it **is** included in the subject's own export because the subject is entitled to know what we pay them.
- **Data Protection Act ¬ß40 ‚Äî right to erasure** (referenced from Chapter 3). `ErasureService` is the redaction workflow. Statutory holds are reported on the `tombstone_log`. Soft delete on the Employees table is **not** erasure ‚Äî clicking Delete only removes a row from active lists; the ¬ß40 workflow requires the formal Erasure request through the Privacy module.
- **Data Protection Act ¬ß44 ‚Äî security-breach notification.** Act 843 ¬ß44 obliges the controller to notify the Data Protection Commission of any security compromise affecting personal data. **There is no breach-notification workflow in the MVP.** The audit chain (Chapter 24) detects tamper attempts on the audit log and notifies super_admins by mail, which is a precursor to a breach-response capability but is not itself a ¬ß44 notification record. This is the largest single gap in the privacy module and is on the Phase 4 roadmap below.
- **Cross-border transfer controls (Act 843 ¬ß47).** The MVP runs in a single jurisdiction; there is no cross-border data-transfer workflow because there is no place to transfer to. When the institute deploys cloud storage outside Ghana (Microsoft 365 EU regions, Google Drive multi-region), the integration metadata in `integrations` records the provider region but does not yet capture an adequacy-decision flag ‚Äî that is also Phase 4 work.
- **Data Protection Commission registration evidence.** The DPC operates a controller-registration register under Act 843 ¬ß46. **The MVP does not carry any DPC registration metadata in code** ‚Äî no `dpc_registration_number`, no `dpc_renewal_date`, no DPO contact block in the public-facing footer beyond what is hard-coded in Vue. An organisation deploying CIHRMS will need to display its DPC certificate separately; the codebase has no opinion about where. This is a known documentation rather than a code gap.
- **GDPR parity (EU 2016/679).** The six rights mirror Articles 15 (access), 16 (rectification), 17 (erasure), 18 (restriction ‚Äî closest analogue is our Objection), 20 (portability), and 21 (object to processing). The thirty-day SLA matches Article 12(3). The records-of-processing audit trail satisfies Article 30. There is no Data Protection Impact Assessment workflow (Article 35) in the MVP. There is no breach-notification workflow (Article 33's seventy-two-hour clock), tracking the same gap as Act 843 ¬ß44 above.
- **Cybersecurity Act, 2020 (Act 1038).** The Cyber Security Authority requires significant security incidents to be notified ‚Äî the same gap as above applies. The audit-chain verification command (`audit:verify-chain --notify`, scheduled daily at 03:00) is the closest existing capability.
- **ISO/IEC 27001 Annex A.18.1 ‚Äî compliance with legal requirements.** The privacy module is the principal artefact for A.18.1.4 ("privacy and protection of personally identifiable information"). The per-request audit trail, the tamper-evident export hashes, and the statutory-hold reporting are the controls. The forward chapter on standards (Chapter 44) maps every control to a section here.

Forward to Chapter 44 for the full standards-benchmark matrix.

## What's planned next

Five things are on the Phase 4 roadmap (see the gap analysis and Chapter 46):

1. **Breach-notification workflow.** A `breach_incidents` table + a `BreachReport` lifecycle (`detected ‚Üí triaged ‚Üí notified_dpc ‚Üí resolved`) with a seventy-two-hour SLA target, the equivalent of the existing DPO queue but for ¬ß44 / GDPR Art. 33 events. The chain-verification job already runs daily; it will gain a "raise a draft breach incident" outcome when a mismatch is detected.
2. **DPC registration metadata in code.** A small `compliance_registrations` table for the institute's DPC certificate number, renewal date, and DPO contact block, surfaced in the public site footer and the case-file header so subjects can verify they are dealing with a registered controller. Once landed, this also feeds the Standards chapter's evidence pack.
3. **Schedule the nightly overdue sweep.** `DataSubjectRequestService::markOverdueRequests()` is implemented and tested, but the call is **not yet wired into `routes/console.php`** ‚Äî today an open request only flips to `overdue` when someone visits the DPO queue and the `is_overdue()` predicate runs in-resource. A `Schedule::call` entry at 00:10 daily will close that gap. (The function returns the row count so the same scheduler can also gold-line an alert when an overdue spike happens.)
4. **Retention policies + auto-purge.** Today no policy auto-purges anything ‚Äî soft-deleted employees, applicants who withdrew, terminated users' tokens, expired payment intents (with one finance-side exception that runs nightly) all accumulate indefinitely. A `data_retention_policies` table keyed by `table_name + statute + retention_years` with a console command `privacy:purge-expired` will give the DPO a daily redaction pass for everything outside a statutory hold. Erasure exports' own ZIP files also need a TTL ‚Äî at present they live in `storage/app/dsr-exports/{reference}.zip` forever.
5. **Subject-side sidebar nav.** Today the `/privacy/my` page has no sidebar entry ‚Äî subjects reach it through the Profile portal (Chapter 16) or by direct URL. A "My Privacy" tile in the user-menu dropdown will close the discoverability gap so every employee knows the workflow exists, not just the ones who already know to look.

Phase 4 work brings the privacy module from compliant-on-the-rights-themselves to compliant-end-to-end, including the institute's own ¬ß44 / ¬ß46 obligations as a controller.
